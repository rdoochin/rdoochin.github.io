When looking at the previous graphics, some interesting conclusions can be drawn. Firstly, the a declining population in the New York burrows is not a new phenomenon. It has happened several times in recent history. Firstly, throughout the 1970s New York was plagued by economic stagnation, rising crime rates, and a fleeting population. The five counties suffered from diminished tax revenue (find data) and teetered on the edge of bankruptcy.

Population started to rebound in the city throughout the 1980s leading to a construction boom (find data), although it diminished in the late 1980s. Black Monday plagued the financial markets in 1987, marking the largest single day percentage decline in the stock market. This event shook the markets and was a catalyst for a decline in real estate prices in the early 1990s. This trend is reflected by the trough in population (demand for real estate) on the scatterplots above. In many counties, crime rates peaked at around point.

The woes of the late 1980s and early 1990s were washed away by an "urban renewal" in New York throughout the 1990s. Prices rebounded as people migrated en masse back the the five burrows, as evidenced by the population data graphics. Once population starts to rebound, economic activity returned to New York and tax revenue increased (find data).

Another interesting trend to note is the gentrification of Kings County (Brooklyn) following the Great Recession. This is reflected in the widening difference between the mean and the median home prices in the area. This reflects the data becoming more right skewed

Interestingly, both population and prices were seemingly diminishing towards the latter part of the 2010s. Rather than being the core reason behind a flight from New York, perhaps COVID is only accelerating a trend that was
